# Re-engagement Playbook

For users who were active but have gone quiet, the goal is to bring them back with a reason that's relevant to *them* — not a generic "come back" nudge. Re-engagement is the highest-leverage lifecycle campaign because the user already knows the product; you're removing friction, not teaching from scratch.

## Segment by recency

Treat "lapsed" as a spectrum, not a single state:

- **Recently lapsed (roughly 7–14 days inactive):** a light, specific nudge is usually enough. Remind them of something in progress or something new.
- **Moderately lapsed (15–30 days):** lead with concrete value and consider surfacing a feature they haven't tried.
- **Long-dormant (30+ days):** a stronger value reminder, and possibly an incentive. These users may need re-onboarding, not just a nudge.

Define recency explicitly against the dataset's as-of date — for example, "no `app_open` in the last 14 days" — and be careful to **exclude users who are still active**. Targeting active users with a "we miss you" message is the most common and most damaging re-engagement mistake.

## Anchor on past behavior

- Remind them of a feature they used or a task they started ("Your draft campaign is still waiting").
- Surface a relevant feature they *haven't* tried — feature discovery is one of the most effective re-engagement angles, especially when the feature maps to why they signed up.
- Avoid pitching features they already use heavily; it signals you don't know them.

## Recommended message shape

1. A short, specific hook tied to their history.
2. A single concrete next step.
3. Keep push to ~2 sentences; in-app and email can be slightly longer.

## Cadence

- Send one nudge, then back off. Do not repeatedly message a user who isn't responding — escalate channel (in-app → push → email) rather than frequency.
- Suppress users who recently received a similar campaign (see frequency capping).
- If a re-engagement series gets no response after 2–3 touches, stop and let the user rest.
