# Brand Voice & Tone

Our messaging voice is **helpful, concise, and human** — like a knowledgeable peer, not a marketer shouting. Every message should read as if a thoughtful product person wrote it specifically for this user.

## Core principles

- **Lead with the value to the user, not the feature name.** "Pick up where you left off" beats "Try Journeys 2.0". Users care about outcomes, not our product taxonomy.
- **Be brief.** Push and in-app copy should be skimmable in under three seconds. If a sentence can be cut without losing meaning, cut it.
- **Use plain language.** Avoid jargon, internal product names, and acronyms the user wouldn't recognize.
- **One idea, one call to action.** A message that asks for two things usually gets neither.
- **Be specific.** "Your weekly report is ready" outperforms "You have new updates."

## Tone calibration

The tone should shift slightly by context while staying recognizably ours:

- **Onboarding / new users:** warm, encouraging, low-pressure. Celebrate small wins.
- **Re-engagement:** friendly and useful, never guilt-tripping.
- **Transactional / important:** clear and direct; drop the flourish.
- **Win-back of long-churned users:** respectful and value-forward; assume they've forgotten us.

## Words and patterns to avoid

- All-caps, excessive punctuation ("!!!"), or manufactured urgency ("ACT NOW", "LAST CHANCE") unless the urgency is real.
- Guilt or shame ("We miss you… did we do something wrong?", "You've been ignoring us").
- Over-promising or implying benefits we can't guarantee.
- Clickbait that the destination doesn't deliver on.
- Negging or fake scarcity.

## Personalization

- Reference what the user actually did — a feature they used, a task they started, where they left off — rather than generic greetings.
- Keep personalization truthful and grounded in real data. Never invent a user's history.
- Never expose internal data, segment names, or the fact that the user was "targeted." Messages should feel like a helpful nudge, not a marketing operation.

## Emoji

At most one, and only when it adds meaning or warmth. Emoji are not a substitute for clear copy, and a wall of them reads as spam.
